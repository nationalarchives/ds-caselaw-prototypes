Mr Justice Meade:

INTRODUCTION

1.

In this action, the Claimant (“Siemens”) alleges that the Defendants (together, “GE”) have infringed European Patent (UK) No. 2 657 519 B1 (“the Patent”). GE denies infringement and say that the Patent is invalid.

2.

The subject matter of the action is wind turbines. GE is undertaking the provision of wind turbines for off-shore electricity generation on Dogger Bank. Its product is called the Haliade-X wind turbine. Turbines of this kind are mighty machines: the blades are of the order of 100 metres long.

3.

There is a counterpart to this action in relation to which GE is suing Siemens for patent infringement. That action is for trial next year.

4.

There have been parallel proceedings in the US where a related patent was found valid and infringed. Contrary to GE’s somewhat prickly expectations, Siemens did not say that the decision was relevant to this trial.

CONDUCT OF THE TRIAL

5.

The trial was conducted live in Court and there were no COVID issues.

6.

Mr Purvis KC undertook the oral advocacy for Siemens. Mr Mitcheson KC undertook it for GE, with the exception of the issues on Dogger Bank territoriality and kit of parts, which were covered by Dr Baran.

THE ISSUES

7.

The issues are:

i)

The scope of the common general knowledge (“CGK”). There were a number of areas of disagreement.

ii)

A number of points on claim interpretation. These go mainly to infringement although GE says they run as squeezes against validity. The arguments included reliance on the file history.

iii)

Infringement, as to which the sub-issues were:

a)

Whether the Haliade-X as fully assembled would fall within the claims of the Patent.

b)

Whether the rotor hub of the Haliade-X on its own falls within claim 9 of the Patent.

c)

Whether Dogger Bank, which is the only site of an alleged infringing act at which the Haliade-X will be fully assembled, is within the territorial scope of the Patents Act 1977 (I refer to it below as “the Act” but also in the context of the territoriality point by its full name).

d)

Whether the unassembled components of the Haliade-X, when in Able Seaton Port in the UK, infringe on the basis of a “kit of parts” argument.

iv)

Validity over two pieces of prior art, each relied on for anticipation and obviousness:

a)

US Patent Application US 2010/0296763 A1, “Loeschner”.

b)

An article entitled “Review of direct-drive radial flux wind turbine generator mechanical design”, Stander J.N. et al., Wind. Energ. 2012; 15; 459-472 “Stander”.

v)

An insufficiency allegation.

8.

The following were dropped in GE’s written closing:

i)

An additional construction point on “at least one bearing”;

ii)

A point that the Haliade-X is, for the purposes of the Act, made in France when its rotor hub is put together, and supplied there to the customer;

iii)

An insufficiency said to arise from ambiguity of the claims;

iv)

A further piece of prior art called “Hua” (Chinese Patent Application CN 102121457A).

9.

Further, Siemens had applied to amend the Patent (and GE opposed the application on numerous grounds) but withdrew that application during its oral closing submissions, saying that it was no longer needed after GE dropped Hua.

10.

Finally, GE pleaded that some of its acts to date, even if they would otherwise infringe a valid claim of the Patent, are covered by the defence of experimental use in s. 60(5)(b) of the Act. It seemed to me that this was unlikely ever to matter, since the real dispute between the parties lies in the ultimate commercial use of the Haliade-X which it is accepted will not be entitled to that defence. At my suggestion, each party agreed that the issue can be deferred until the question of relief arises, if it ever does.

THE WITNESSES

11.

In relation to the facts, GE called Mr Vincent Schellings to verify the PPD. He was cross-examined briefly but very little turned on it. He was a good witness.

12.

Each side called one expert.

Dr Santos

13.

Siemens’ expert was Dr Richard Santos. GE made no personal criticism of him, and accepted that he had industry experience, but said it was too greatly focussed on certification testing and control issues. As a result, GE, argued, he envisaged a skilled person who was unrealistically cautious and would not advance proposals unless they were based on turbines which had a very long track record. This is really a point about the skilled person so I deal with it there.

14.

I found Dr Santos to be a good and fair witness in general. He may not have perfectly embodied the skilled person himself but that is not a requirement and subject to what I say below, he was able to put himself in the position of the notional skilled person quite well.

Prof Slocum

15.

GE’s expert was Prof Alexander Slocum of MIT. Siemens made a number of criticisms of him:

i)

That he was, both personally and in his conception of the skilled person, too focussed on large bearings for precision applications and not enough on wind turbines. It is true that that has been a large part of his personal experience but (a) the Patent is very largely about bearings and the forces that act on them, (b) he knew about wind turbines himself from extensive practical experience, and (c) he envisaged the skilled person knowing about them to an appropriate degree and while perhaps he saw that knowledge coming from reading rather than experience to a surprising degree, it did not matter.

ii)

That he was not transparent about the research he did to found his views on the CGK and had an inappropriately liberal or expansive view of what was CGK. As to the first, I did not think it mattered in practical terms that he e.g. did a Google Scholar search to avoid “weird stuff”. As to the second, I think it is true that he set the bar for CGK rather low, and I take that into account when I assess, in particular, Stander and the designs it references. But it had no impact on his evidence as to what would be obvious to do once one has identified the CGK.

iii)

That his explanation of the prior art was hard to follow and that he was applying the standard of what he would do, rather than what the notional, uninventive skilled person would do. I reject both of these. Siemens said that Prof Slocum accepted the latter point, but read as a whole I do not think that is a fair view of his oral evidence.

iv)

That he has given expert evidence many times and is an inventive person. Both these things are true, but they are very common in patent cases and what one has to consider is whether they had an adverse effect. In the present case I do not think they did and I reject the suggestion that they lessened the usefulness of his evidence.

16.

Thus I reject all the criticisms apart from the one concerning the standard for CGK which is not a personal criticism of Prof Slocum and which I will take into account below. Overall I thought he was an excellent witness.

THE SKILLED PERSON

17.

There was no real dispute about the identity of the skilled person in broad terms. They would need knowledge of mechanical, structural and electrical engineering and practical experience of wind turbines and their main components, including in particular their bearings. Given this range of disciplines it is probable that a team rather than one individual would be needed in real life, but nothing turns on this and I will just refer to “the skilled person”.

18.

There are two matters concerning the attitudes of the skilled person that were disputed and that I consider relevant. They might perhaps be seen as facets of CGK although they do not concern specific, concrete information.

19.

The first concerns the degree of conservatism of the skilled person. There seemed to be agreement, and I find, that this was a field in which safety was very important and that wind turbine designs would be understood to have to meet very demanding standards of durability and reliability. To give a flavour of this, Dr Santos referred frequently to the need for a design to be such that it could be predicted to be in operation for 95% of the time over 20 years. I accept that that was generally seen as desirable and that this was a demanding field in which to work as a result.

20.

However, Dr Santos went further and said that the skilled person would not be interested in progressing any design until he or she could be sure that it had already accrued a very long period of successful use in practice and in multiple locations. GE submitted that he had said that a design would need a 20-year track record. I do not think he did, and there may have been some misunderstanding, but certainly he said that a very substantial period of successful use was seen as necessary and Siemens made that part of its case.

21.

This point is primarily relevant to which of the proposed designs in Stander the skilled person would be interested in progressing and it was in that context that much of the cross-examination took place. Prof Slocum rejected the strict view put forward by Siemens.

22.

I do not accept Dr Santos’ black-and-white position or Siemens’ arguments based on it. I prefer the more flexible attitude put forward by Prof Slocum; more established designs would have that in their favour but newer, or even untested ones, would be assessed on the merits while taking into account their relative lack of use. Quite apart from anything else, if Dr Santos were right then new designs in the industry would have come along at a snail’s pace, which does not seem to have been the case. Further, the industry needed to react to changing external requirements as they arose. Ever-bigger turbines were needed, yet there was no sign that the players in the field hesitated to set about designing them.

23.

The second matter concerns the approach to designing wind turbines. In the Annex to his first report, Prof Slocum presented an analysis of forces, moments, deflections and so on for a rotating shaft support bearing, and it was at this general level of complexity that he looked at the prior art. He referred to it in his oral evidence as the “teeter-totter”, which is the American English word for seesaw. I will use it as a convenient shorthand, although the mechanical arrangement in a wind turbine has more elements than a simple seesaw.

24.

Dr Santos said that no wind turbine would ever be designed on such a simplistic basis and that much more complex computer analyses were needed in real life.

25.

I do not think the experts’ views were in fact inconsistent. Prof Slocum was talking about a first-cut but scientifically sound analysis that would be undertaken at the beginning of a design process or to assess an existing or proposed design at a high level. Dr Santos was talking about the later, detailed design for actually advancing a real turbine towards production and use. I find that Prof Slocum’s level of analysis would be applied to understand the pros and cons of designs at the sort of level of detail given in the prior art and, indeed, the Patent. His level of analysis would, for example, support an understanding of the correctness of the “key idea” at [0010] of the Patent.

STATEMENT OF AGREED CGK

Introduction to wind turbines

1.

Wind turbines comprise the following basic parts:

(1)

the rotor blades: these are aerodynamically shaped blades which, when the wind is incident on the blades, generates a torque that causes the rotor hub (to which the blades are attached) to spin [Santos 1, para 81; Slocum 1, para 40]. Modern wind turbines generally have three blades which are attached to a rotor hub positioned upwind (i.e. in-front relative to the incident wind direction) of the tower.

(2)

the rotor hub: the rotor blades are attached to the rotor hub which is itself connected to the generator rotor which is rotatably connected to the stationary part of the generator [Santos 1, paras 80-81; Slocum 1, para 40];

(3)

the generator (or electrical generator): the generator comprises at least two parts a rotating rotor (the generator rotor not to be confused with the rotor i.e. the rotor hub and blades) and a stationary generator stator. The generator rotor contains the magnets (either coils that act as electromagnets once electrically excited, or permanent magnets) and the stator contains stator coils (or windings) in which a voltage is induced via electromagnetic induction [Santos 1, para 84;

Slocum 1, para 40];

(4)

the tower: the tower supports the nacelle, generator, rotor hub, and blades, and will be secured in place by a foundation [Santos 1, para 85; Slocum 1, para 40].

2.

Incident kinetic energy from the wind on the rotor blades is turned into mechanical energy as the rotor blades rotate. This mechanical energy, in turn, is converted into electricity via a generator [Santos 1, para 35; Slocum 1, para 40].

Direct drive vs. geared wind turbines

3.

Wind turbines can be geared or direct drive [Santos 1, para 77; Slocum 1, paras 41-44].

4.

Geared wind turbines have a gearbox located between the rotor hub and the generator which means that the generator rotor speed is faster than the slow rotational speed of the rotor hub [Santos 1, para 78; Slocum 1, para 42]. Direct drive turbines have a direct (i.e. one-to-one) connection between the rotor hub and the generator rotor (so that they rotate together) i.e. they have no gearbox [Santos 1, para 89; Slocum 1, para 43].

5.

Although geared wind turbines can have a lower generator cost, the gearbox is often the highest-maintenance component of these turbines, the weak-point of the drive train and the part that has caused the most significant downtime [Santos 1, para 87; Slocum 1, para 42]. For direct drive turbines, the rotational speed of the generator rotor is low and the torque is high which necessitates a different type of generator design [Santos 1, para 78; Slocum 1, para 43], so the generator diameter tends to be large. 7.

Although geared wind turbines are still being used, and represented the majority of installed wind turbines (both offshore and onshore) at the Priority Date, direct-drive turbines were (at the Priority Date) gaining in popularity [Slocum 1, para 44; Santos 2, para 19].

Offshore vs. onshore turbines

8.

Wind turbines can be used to generate electricity both onshore and offshore.

9.

At the Priority Date the offshore wind turbine market was rapidly expanding [Santos 1, para 104].

10.

Onshore wind turbines are generally simpler to install and maintain compared to offshore wind turbines [Slocum 1, para 41].

The drive train

11.

All wind turbines rely on the same principle of turning kinetic energy from the wind into mechanical energy and then finally into electrical energy via a generator. The system in the turbine which facilitates this energy conversion process is referred to as the drive train [Santos 1, para 75].

Development of wind turbines

12.

Throughout the 1980s, the power rating and size of commercial wind turbines remained small. Rotors were approximately 20 m in diameter and the turbines had a capacity of 100 kW. However, the power rating and physical size of turbines increased considerably between the 1980s and 2010 and this trend has continued [Santos 1, para 39].

13.

A wind turbine is designed to continuously operate for at least 20 years when properly maintained. [Santos 1, para 40].

14.

Wind turbine designs are required to comply with standards such as the Germanischer Lloyd Guideline for the Certification of Wind Turbines and the IEC61400-1:2005 standard [Santos 1, para 29; Santos 2, footnotes 5 & 8 ].

Physical concepts applicable to wind turbine design

Forces

15.

A force (or load) is an influence that can change the motion of an object. A force has a magnitude and a direction. An applied force is a force that is applied to the mass of a structure. In the context of wind turbines, such an applied force can result from the pressure of the wind acting on an area of the turbine, or from the mass of a component due to gravity [Santos 1, para 41].

16.

A reaction force (or load) is a force that counteracts an applied force. If the reaction force is equal in magnitude and opposite in direction to the applied force then the force will be balanced. If not the forces will be unbalanced. Unbalanced forces lead to acceleration or deceleration of the mass affected by the forces [Santos 1, para 42].

17.

The moment of force (or moment rotate about an axis. A moment is the product of the force and the perpendicular distance between the force and an axis [Santos 1, para 43].

18.

A force couple is two forces of equal and opposite magnitude separated by a distance which act on an object to generate a moment or react against a moment. [Santos 1, para 44].

19.

In the wind turbine industry, there is a convention that ‘torque’ is used to describe the in- plane moment around the central longitudinal axis of the turbine caused by the rotation of the rotor blades and rotor hub to which they are attached (i.e. the moment in the plane of rotation) [Santos 1, para 45], whereas ‘moment’ is used to describe moments acting outside the plane of rotation [Santos 1, para 46].

20.

Wind turbines, particularly large wind turbines, are subject to a number of different forces including:

(1)

dynamic (or aerodynamic) forces which vary with the wind and the movement of the turbine [Santos 1, para 47; Slocum 1 para 49]. These include:

(a)

The in-plane moment (torque) that causes the generator to turn.

(b)

the asymmetric moments acting on the rotor which include the yawing moments (acting in the x-z plane) and the pitching moments (acting in the y- z plane) (as shown below) [Santos 1, para 47 & Fig. 7]; and

(c)

thrust forces which vary with wind speed and the pitch of the turbine blades (i.e. the angle the blades make with the incident wind field) and act to push the rotor backwards in a direction parallel to the z axis (i.e. in the axial direction) [Santos 1, para 47; Slocum 1 para 50]; and

(2)

static moments that remain unchanged over time. These are gravity induced moments created where the centre of mass of a turbine component is located an axial distance away from a pivot point e.g. the weight of the rotor hub and the blades creates a downward radial force aligned with gravity a perpendicular distance away from a pivot point [Santos 1, para 48; Slocum 1 paras 49 & 50].

21.

The direction of the axis around which the blades spin is known as the axial direction. The direction along the radius of the bearing is known as the radial direction [Slocum 1, para 50].

24.

A general aim of wind turbine design is therefore to reduce the loads that can cause the structure to fail from long term fatigue as much as possible [Slocum 1, para 49].

25.

Loads on a wind turbine (and the main bearings of a wind turbine) come from a variety of sources. Aerodynamic loads come from the incident wind field, vertical wind shear, tower interference, cross wind on the rotor, turbine wake (see further below) [Santos 1, paras 8- 103; Slocum 1, Fig. 5].

Electromagnetic induction

26.

Permanent magnets (PMs) and electromagnets generate a magnetic field [Santos 1, para 51].

27.

The strength of the magnetic field in a given area is the magnetic flux. When a conductor is subjected to a change in magnetic flux, an electromotive force (a voltage) is induced in proportion to the rate of change of magnetic flux:

h

28.

Electromagnetic induction is the principle utilised in alternating current (AC) generators whereby conducting coils rotate within a stationary magnetic field (or the magnetic field moves relative to the conducting coils) [Santos 1, para 52].

29.

In a direct drive wind turbine the generator rotor contains magnets, and the generator stator contains windings (also known as coils). The relative motion between the lines of magnetic flux of the rotating magnets and the windings induces an electric current in the windings .Wind turbines thus generate electricity when the wind causes the blades to spin the rotor hub, which in turn rotates the generator rotor relative to a generator stator: when the rotor magnets move past the stator coils, electricity is generated [Slocum 1, para 40].

30.

A larger electromotive force (and hence the generation of more electric power), can be induced in a wind turbine generator as follows [Santos 1, para 55]:

(1)

using more powerful magnets and/or minimising the space between the magnets and the conducting coils (the air gap) to increase the magnetic flux density at the windings;

(2)

increasing the speed at which either the conducting coils or the magnets rotate relative to each other. This may be achieved using a gearbox which turns high torque, low speed power from the rotor into low torque, high speed power to be input into the generator rotor;

(3)

increasing the diameter of the generator rotor in order to increase the number of magnetic poles in the generator.

(4)

making the generator axially longer to increase the area in which the electromotive force (i.e. voltage) is generated.

Bearings

31.

A bearing is a machine element that supports motion (most relevantly in wind turbines, rotation) of components with respect to one another with a minimal amount of frictional resistance [Santos 1, para 56; Slocum 1, para 45].

32.

Different bearings are capable of supporting different loads (axial, radial, moment). The choice of bearing(s) will depend on the loads to be supported and the overall design of the wind turbine.

Functions of bearings in a wind turbine

33.

Wind turbines have several bearings in their architecture including: a yaw bearing at the top of the tower in order for the yaw mechanism to rotate the nacelle, generator and rotor hub around the vertical (tower) axis so the rotor hub may be aligned with the wind; a pitch bearing for each rotor blade to allow a blade’s profile presented to the wind to be changed in order to control the rotation speed of the hub; and at least one main bearing that allows the blades and rotor hub to rotate about the horizontal axis to turn and generate power [Santos 1, para 56].

34.

The yaw bearings are not designed to continuously rotate. The yaw and pitch bearings support large loads, but rarely complete an entire rotation and are often referred to as slewing bearings (or slewing rings). [Santos 1, para 57; Slocum 1, para 76; Santos 2, para 53].

35.

The use of bearings, including their design and configuration, is context-specific. How bearings may form part of the overall turbine design will depend on various aspects of the turbine architecture [Santos 1, para 58].

36.

The main bearing(s) in a direct drive wind turbine have two primary functional requirements:

(1)

support the parts that turn (generator rotor, rotating main shaft and rotor hub (the rotor hub includes the blades and the blade pitch control mechanism)) with very low frictional resistance in order to help maximise system efficiency [Santos 1, para 95; Slocum 1, para 46]; and

(2)

maintain accurate motion of the generator rotor with respect to the stator and, in particular, maintain the air gap (a small radial gap between the generator rotor magnets and the stator coils [Santos 1, para 95; Slocum 1, para 48].

37.

A small air gap helps with generator efficiency because the magnetic field that the coils experience is stronger when the air gap is smaller. Variations in the air gap can cause a variation in the power being generated. If the air gap decreases on one side while increasing on the other side of the rotor the radial pull (attraction force) between the magnets and the coils increases on that one side where the air gap has decreased; as a consequence, this can further decrease the air gap with additional detrimental effects including potentially the magnets and coils touching and causing catastrophic failure. It is therefore important for the bearing to minimize variations in the air gap by caused by the yawing and pitching moments. This is achieved using a bearing configuration with a high degree of stiffness. [Santos 1, paras 55.1 & 67; Slocum 1, para 48 & 121].

Rotor Hubs

38.

The rotor hub is normally cast as a single piece of ductile iron [Santos 1, para 80].

39.

When a rotor hub is spherically shaped, the torque and the resultant stresses are transmitted most efficiently [Slocum 1, para 87; Santos 2, para 58].

40.

Rotor hubs have holes in them for the rotor blades to be attached [Slocum 2, para 39].

Disputed CGK

28.

Some of the issues on CGK fell away. I have worked from the parties’ written and oral closings to identify those still live.

29.

A number concerning the CGK status of the real designs of companies known as Vensys or Gen[e]sys coalesced into a single issue and I address it as such below.

Disputed issue 2 – conventional way to indicate a rolling element bearing

30.

This point goes to the allegation of anticipation by Stander.

31.

I find that it was, as a matter of CGK, conventional to represent a rolling element bearing with the symbol ☒, as GE alleges. There were numerous examples in the papers where it was used in that way, and none where it was used to denote something different. Prof Slocum supported this case, and Dr Santos did not truly contradict it, although he said that it was context dependent. However, the place where the context was said to go the other way was only Stander, which I deal with below. In a nutshell, the issue there is not whether the symbol denotes a rolling element bearing in the drawings, but how to assess that in the light of the accompanying text.

Disputed issue 8 – removal of press/shrink fit bearing rings

32.

Although both parties put in written closing submissions on this issue I found it hard to see to what it went to that was still live by the end of trial.

33.

As best I can understand, the point was that if “integral” in claim 1 of the Patent was the antithesis of “removable” (which is not how I understood Siemens to argue it, in fact), then GE said that press fit/shrink fit bearings were removable (without causing damage), and it fortified this point by saying that the Haliade-X bearings were removable, a point which Siemens challenged in the cross-examination of Mr Schellings. How the Haliade-X actually works is not part of the CGK, but I will address it here.

34.

My finding is that in principle it would be possible to remove a press fit/shrink fit bearing without damage by the application of cold or heat as appropriate, but that there was no CGK understanding that it would be as easy as removing a bearing which was bolted in place.

35.

In relation to the Haliade-X my finding is that its bearings could be removed and replaced without damage, but not in situ. Removal would require the whole turbine to be taken down and returned to shore. This has in fact never been done. Siemens relied on a brief extract of the deposition of a GE witness in the US proceedings which it said suggested the contrary, but I think that was reading too much into it and I accept Mr Schellings’ evidence and the other documents.

Disputed issue 9 – single and double bearing configurations

36.

This only in the end went to obviousness of dependent claim 6 over Loeschner if claim 1 was obvious over that citation. I hold below that claim 1 was not obvious over Loeschner but that if it had been then so would claim 6. Neither my finding nor Siemens’ defence of the claim turned on this aspect of CGK. So I will just very briefly say that it was CGK that both single and double bearing arrangements might be used in larger wind turbines, and that the choice was context-dependent.

Vensys/Genesys and design B4 in Stander

37.

This somewhat complex issue (which comprehended disputed CGK issues 1, 4 and part of 9 in the agreed list of disputed points) is closely related to the disclosure of Stander; to whether the skilled person would read Stander knowing that design B4 in it (central to GE’s case) was a real, CGK turbine that was in use; and whether and with what effect the skilled person reading Stander would follow up reference 6 in it, which is called “Klinger”. For an understanding of this dynamic, it is necessary to read the section of this judgment on Stander as well as this section, although of course they relate to different concepts and apply different standards. I remind myself that at this stage I am assessing CGK, to the standard that requires, and not what Stander discloses.

38.

GE said that there was a commercial entity called Vensys, that it had turbine products called the Vensys 62, 70 and 77, that it was the brainchild of a Prof Klinger, that Prof Klinger had another company called Gensys that was related to Vensys, and that the turbine company called Goldwind was a licensee of Vensys.

39.

These matters were probably, in point of historical fact, true. GE put in a CEA Notice in order to prove them. It was somewhat unsatisfactory in its lack of precision and clarity about what it intended to prove, but nonetheless, if the issue for me was whether those facts were true, I would hold that on the balance of probabilities, they were. GE relied in particular on:

Stander itself, on the basis that it was in a well-respected industry publication called Wind Energy. I say more about this below.

A book called “Electrical Drives for Direct Drive Renewable Energy Systems” by Mueller and Polinder. However, this was not published until after the priority date.

A presentation delivered by Prof Klinger and three others at the industry conference EWEC in June 2003, which disclosed a two-bearing design called the Vensys 62, which it was said was in operation as a prototype.

However, although I hold those facts to be true, GE was miles from being able to show they were CGK. Its written closing submissions more or less assumed that if there was a document available that stated the facts, they must be CGK. That is of course just not the legal standard. It was telling that GE had to resort to the use of a CEA Notice, which it clearly did because its own witness, Prof Slocum, did not have personal knowledge of the matters relied on and could not speak to them. The use of a post-priority book is also unpromising. I accept that material being presented at an industry conference can be a starting point for CGK and that in some instances key presentations at major meetings can become CGK very quickly, but not every presentation at every meeting is CGK. Merely showing that there was a presentation is just not enough.

So I reject GE’s very ambitious position on this aspect of CGK. On the basis of the experts’ evidence, however, I do hold that in addition to the very big players (Siemens, Enercon etc.), who it was common ground were well-known, it was CGK that there were other smaller players seeking to progress prototypes, and that Vensys was one of them with some small scale use in Europe. Similarly, Goldwind as a company was CGK. But, to be clear, I reject its having been CGK that Vensys had a commercial relationship with Goldwind, and Gen[e]sys as a company was not CGK. I also find that the CGK did not extend to details of what Vensys’ designs were, but only to the fact that it had one or more designs.

CLAIM SCOPE AND INFRINGEMENT

62.

The disputed issues on claim scope all concern the “normal” interpretation of the claims; there is no issue about equivalence. There was no dispute about the legal principles applicable, see Saab Seaeye v Atlas Elektronik [2017] EWCA Civ 2175 at [17]-[18] applying Virgin v Premium [2009] EWCA Civ 1062.

63.

Infringement by equivalence now has to be pleaded in patent cases. This is a salutary and useful practice, but it has led to it being common for the alleged infringer to make objections, where equivalence is not pleaded, that the patentee is limited in what can be argued in support of “normal” interpretation. This happens in particular where the patentee seeks to rely on how the invention works and what it is trying to accomplish. These objections are generally misconceived, in my view. “Normal” interpretation includes purposive matters of this kind. Equivalence arises on top of normal interpretation and that is what has to be pleaded. Thus in the present case GE argued, at least at one stage, that Siemens’ arguments about how press-fitting works were not allowed because they were unpleaded. I do not agree (and the objection faded rather) – the issue was “normal” interpretation of a general word in context. Siemens was not running equivalence.

Issue 1 – Integral

64.

This point arises on feature (d) of claim 1 and feature (d) of claim 9. Siemens said that it covered press-fit connections and GE said that it was limited to situations where the annular member and bearing outer ring are of one piece; truly monolithic. It was common ground that if the annular member and bearing outer ring were bolted together then the feature was not satisfied.

Ordinary meaning

65.

It was common ground that “integral” is an ordinary English word and not a term of art. However, it is an ordinary English word which can bear multiple different meanings. Its ordinary usage can extend to that which is an undivided whole (GE's contention, essentially), or to something made up of components which form a unity (closer to Siemens’ position). So context is key.

Technical background facts

66.

In a bolted connection there have to be holes to receive the bolts and there have to be bolts, which are separate components. This is trivial but worth identifying. What is less obvious is that the forces transmitted through such a connection when the turbine is working are not transmitted through the bolts but by friction between the mating surfaces, albeit that the compressive force giving rise to the frictional force comes from the bolts. This was not in dispute.

67.

In a press-fit connection there are of course no separate components. There are just the two pieces in a very intimate connection, pressed against each other when one of the components expands or contracts when the heating or cooling ceases (as the case may be).

68.

These two different approaches give different load paths, as Dr Santos showed in the following diagram:

69.

GE objected that load paths are not mentioned in the specification. I do not see the relevance of that. This is basic mechanics that would readily occur to the skilled person if they were thinking about whether a press fit was “integral” and/or how it differed from a bolted connection.

70.

The experts agreed, however, that a bolted connection and a press-fit connection, if carefully and properly implemented, will both work well.

71.

Siemens put to Prof Slocum one of his own publications which said that in a well-designed press-fit connection “the materials behave as if they were monolithic”. This obviously helps Siemens, but later Prof Slocum explained that there remains a difference between a press-fit connection and a truly monolithic unit, which is that the former means there will be an interface where there is a high stress concentration. In the bearing field it was known that friction at this interface could lead to an undesirable form of wear called “fretting”.

72.

A further point is that where press-fits were used in the context of bearings, it was usual to have a shoulder for extra protection against relative movement caused by loads in the axial direction. This would not be necessary with a truly monolithic unit.

“Removable” as the antithesis of “integral”

73.

I have mentioned this above in connection with the CGK. The skilled person would think that a bearing held in place by a press-fit connection could be removed, but less easily than a bolted connection. I do not think this has any material impact on the meaning of “integral”. It is at most another facet of the fact that using a press-fit comes with some complications.

Context

74.

One part of the context, as I will develop further when I address the file history, is that “integral” would be understood to be a more stringent requirement than merely “firmly connected” and would not cover bolted connections. The reason for using the former and not the latter in the claims was to provide a distinction over the prior art and in particular Loeschner. In my view the core of the invention is to do with moving the bearing into the hub, and accomplishing that with the required annular member. The connection of the outer ring to the annular member is not presented as being of any real significance to the invention.

75.

Further context is to be found in the specification, but the pointers are only minor ones. I was referred to the following paragraphs:

ii)

Reliance on the prosecution history

77.

Both sides referred to Actavis v. Lilly [2017] UKSC 48, and to [87]-[88] in particular:

87.  In my judgment, it is appropriate for the UK courts to adopt a sceptical, but not absolutist, attitude to a suggestion that the contents of the prosecution file of a patent should be referred to when considering a question of interpretation or infringement, along substantially the same lines as the German and Dutch courts. It is tempting to exclude the file on the basis that anyone concerned about, or affected by, a patent should be entitled to rely on its contents without searching other records such as the prosecution file, as a matter of both principle and practicality. However, given that the contents of the file are publicly available (by virtue of article 128 EPC 2000) and (at least according to what we were told) are unlikely to be extensive, there will be occasions when justice may fairly be said to require reference to be made to the contents of the file. However, not least in the light of the wording of article 69 EPC 2000, which is discussed above, the circumstances in which a court can rely on the prosecution history to determine the extent of protection or scope of a patent must be limited.

88.  While it would be arrogant to exclude the existence of any other circumstances, my current view is that reference to the file would only be appropriate where (i) the point at issue is truly unclear if one confines oneself to the specification and claims of the patent, and the contents of the file unambiguously resolve the point, or (ii) it would be contrary to the public interest for the contents of the file to be ignored. The first type of circumstance is, I hope, self-explanatory; the second would be exemplified by a case where the patentee had made it clear to the EPO that he was not seeking to contend that his patent, if granted, would extend its scope to the sort of variant which he now claims infringes.

78.

In my view these two paragraphs have to be read as a piece. The two possible circumstances referred to in [88] as (i) and (ii) have to be read in the light of the scepticism expressed at the start of [87]; one expects that they will only rarely arise and that the conditions for them to do so will be stringent.

79.

This is given effect as to (i) by the requirements that the point of interpretation has to be truly unclear and that the file unambiguously resolves the point. The first requirement will not be met merely because some point of interpretation is arguable both ways in the context of the specification; that happens all the time. More is needed. The second requires that the file provides a clear answer, and will not be met just because the file says something that might be relevant. It is not legitimate to promote the file into a sort of adjunct to the specification and of equal value with it.

80.

As to (ii) the requirements are that the patentee made it clear that he was not seeking to extend the claims to the sort of variant said to infringe. Again, the mere fact that the file provides something that would have been of some potential relevance to claim scope had it been in the specification is not enough.

81.

I record that Counsel for Siemens accepted that it is not only in relation to infringement by equivalence that the file may be relevant; it can also be relevant to normal, purposive infringement.

82.

It was also common ground before me (based on FibroGen v Akebia [2020] EWHC 866 (Pat) and Facebook v Voxer [2021] EWHC 1377 (Pat)) that where claims have been amended the skilled person knows that, and the reason. This is a point about claim interpretation as such, bearing potentially on Actavis question 3, and distinct from reliance on the prosecution history, in particular because it does not open the door to what the patentee said (beyond that it was amending, and the reason in terms of the prior art necessitating it).

Assessment – file history

89.

I do not think that the claims are sufficiently unclear to engage circumstance (i) in the first place. There is plainly a real argument to be had over “integral” but that does not mean that it cannot be resolved in the usual way.

90.

Even if I were wrong about that, I do not think that the file unambiguously resolves the matter. It establishes in relation to Loeschner (D1) that Siemens saw a need to craft the claim to exclude a bolted connection. One can clearly infer that a bolted connection would fall within “firmly connected” (the deleted expression) and would not fall within “integral”, but one cannot resolve the precise boundary of “integral” and one cannot determine whether a press fit would provide it or not.

Counsel for GE made a subtle argument in this connection, along the following lines:

i)

One can see that in relation to the connection of the outer ring of the bearing to the annular member “firmly connected” was removed.

iv)

I would question the logic of this in any event since it would be possible for some intimate form of mating to both be a firm connection and to leave the two components integral with one another (as indeed I hold to be the case), but it is far too complex and latent to meet the requirements Lord Neuberger set out as I have interpreted them.

In relation to both points, the position as to D2 is significantly less clear than for Loeschner (D1). 96.

I reject GE’s reliance on the file history accordingly.

97.

It remains the case that the fact of amendment necessitated by Loeschner is relevant to ordinary claim interpretation, but it does not help GE for like reasons to those I have just covered: the skilled person would know that “firmly connected” had been deleted to avoid covering a bolted connection.

Analysis and conclusion

98.

Against the background set out above (but excluding the file history, for reasons given), the skilled person would think that “integral” meant that the outer ring and the annular member were truly in one piece, or were so closely connected that they behaved for the purposes of transmitting a load as if they were in one piece. Specifically, this would mean that an outer ring and annular member in a press fit would be “integral”.

99.

Bolted connections would not satisfy this. They could provide an entirely satisfactory connection but however good they were they would need components additional to the ring and annular member, and would involve different load paths.

100.

I attach significant weight to Prof Slocum’s expressed view that in a good press fit the components can be considered monolithic. The fact that a press fit can be done badly so as to cause problems at the interface, and even fretting, and might require a shoulder to take axial loads, does not change this.

101.

There is no inconsistency between this result and the fact that the claims were amended to deal with the prior art; the skilled person would conclude that the patentee had done enough to avoid the prior art, and Loeschner in particular, but not more.

102.

As to the fact that the examples still falling within the claim after amendment have journal bearings, that is of at most superficial significance. The patentee had to exclude the examples that were only said to be firmly connected and those happened to be the ones with rolling element bearings. It says nothing about what other forms of connection might be possible or “integral” with rolling element bearings or with journal bearings.

103.

On this interpretation, the Haliade-X satisfies the requirement of the claims as to “integral”, because it has a press-fit connection. I will identify where that is so when I deal with the facts in more detail below, but there was no dispute about the facts.

Claim 9

125.

I have found that the Haliade-X when fully assembled does not have all the features of claim 1, so there can be no infringement of that claim.

126.

Had I reached the opposite conclusion, Siemens would still have faced the problem that the Haliade-X is never fully assembled until put in place on Dogger Bank. Below, I consider whether or not Dogger Bank is within the territorial reach of the Patents Act 1977 and conclude that it is not. Siemens had two further answers to that. The first is reliance on claim 9 of the Patent, and the second is a “kit of parts” argument.

127.

Although phrased somewhat inelegantly and probably slightly in error (“and at least one annular member integral part of a bearing” does not make grammatical sense), the thrust of the claim is clear: it is a claim to the rotor hub of the earlier claims and thus embodies the inventive idea. I have pointed out above that claim 9 is more emphatic than claim 1 in relation to whether the annular member is to be part of the hub, but had I been wrong in my view of the requirement for the annular member to be part of the hub, then the hub (item B2 in figure 2 of the PPD) would have fallen within claim 9 even when not in an assembled turbine.

128.

GE presented no real answer to this. It pointed out that Siemens had not said that claim 9 was independently inventive of claim 1. That is true, but irrelevant. Siemens was not relying on it for that purpose, but because it is broader for the purposes of infringement, as it does not require all the components of a turbine, only the hub. GE also argued that Dr Santos had said in the context of claim 9 that the annular member and the bearing were “one and the same” so truly monolithic, but this was just cherry-picking and “integral” means the same in claims 1 and 9.

129.

Although Siemens’ action fails because of obviousness over Stander and because of the “of the rotor hub” point on claim scope, that does not mean that there is anything illegitimate about claim 9. On the contrary, it is a rational approach by a patentee to an invention which lies in only part of the overall product and in relation to which the complete product could well be made or kept (as to all of its individual components) but not fully assembled in a given jurisdiction.

134.

At the stage of written opening submissions, there was a misunderstanding about what was in dispute. GE thought that Siemens’ argument was that any acts done in an area designated by the CSA64 were within the scope of s. 132(4) of the Patents Act 1977, whether or not done for an activity falling within s. 11(2) of the PA98. GE said that it would have been a breach of the UK’s obligations under international law to extend the Patents Act 1977 to the Continental Shelf without limitation of purpose.

135.

In fact, however, Siemens was not saying that. It accepts that what GE called the “acts limitation” of the PA98 applies in respect of both geographical areas (and in fact it was clear that there was an acts limitation in the original s. 132(4) extending the Patents Act 1977 to the Continental Shelf at a time when the PA98 had not entered the picture).

136.

It is therefore necessary to construe s. 11(2)(a) of the PA98 to consider whether the activities to be done at Dogger Bank with the Haliade-X turbines when their towers are installed on the seabed are “activities connected with the exploration of, or the exploitation of the natural resources of, the shore or bed of waters to which this section applies or the subsoil beneath it;”.

137.

The parties’ agreement that the “acts limitation” applies shortens my task considerably. It is still necessary to consider the international law that formed the backdrop to the UK legislation as it provides what I am satisfied is admissible and potentially relevant context for the task of interpreting s. 11(2)(a) PA98.

138.

Of particular importance is the United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea (UNCLOS). I agree with GE that it is necessary to bear in mind that the Continental Shelf is distinct from the Exclusive Economic Zone (“EEZ”). UNCLOS treats them distinctly, although there is some cross-reference, as I will explain.

139.

I was referred to the following provisions of UNCLOS:

140.

Article 56, which is part of Part V, dealing with the EEZ, provides:

Article 56

1.

In the exclusive economic zone, the coastal State has:

(a) sovereign rights for the purpose of exploring and exploiting, conserving and managing the natural resources, whether living or non-living, of the waters superjacent to the seabed and of the seabed and its subsoil, and with regard to other activities for the economic exploitation and exploration of the zone, such as the production of energy from the water, currents and winds;

(iii) the protection and preservation of the marine environment;

(c) other rights and duties provided for in this Convention.

2.

In exercising its rights and performing its duties under this Convention in the exclusive economic zone, the coastal State shall have due regard to the rights and duties of other States and shall act in a manner compatible with the provisions of this Convention.

3.

The rights set out in this article with respect to the seabed and subsoil shall be exercised in accordance with Part VI.

It is important to note that Article 56(1)(a) is in two parts. The coastal State has, first, sovereign rights for purposes which correspond quite closely to the PA98, and then, second (from the words “and with regard”) to some other matters. The State has the ability to exercise those rights, but that does not mean that it has done so, or done so in any particular way.

1.

In the exclusive economic zone, the coastal State shall have the exclusive right to construct and to authorize and regulate the construction, operation and use of:

(a) artificial islands;

(b) installations and structures for the purposes provided for in article 56 and other economic purposes;

(c) installations and structures which may interfere with the exercise of the rights of the coastal State in the zone.

2.

The coastal State shall have exclusive jurisdiction over such artificial islands, installations and structures, including jurisdiction with regard to customs, fiscal, health, safety and immigration laws and regulations.

143.

Article 77, within part VI, now dealing with the Continental Shelf, says:

Article 77

1.

The coastal State exercises over the continental shelf sovereign rights for the purpose of exploring it and exploiting its natural resources.

2.

The rights referred to in paragraph 1 are exclusive in the sense that if the coastal State does not explore the continental shelf or exploit its natural resources, no one may undertake these activities without the express consent of the coastal State.

The natural resources referred to in this Part consist of the mineral and other non-living resources of the seabed and subsoil together with living organisms belonging to sedentary species, that is to say, organisms which, at the harvestable stage, either are immobile on or under the seabed or are unable to move except in constant physical contact with the seabed or the subsoil.

144.

Article 80, still within Part VI, says as follows:

Article 80

Artificial islands, installations and structures on the continental shelf

Article 60 applies mutatis mutandis to artificial islands, installations and structures on the continental shelf.

This therefore cross-applies Article 60, which concerns the EEZ, to, inter alia, structures on the continental shelf. As one saw in Article 60, that referred back in turn to Article 56.

146.

One must note, however, that this cross-referencing does not undermine the point I made earlier that while the coastal State is entitled to exercise its sovereign rights to the full extent provided, to what extent it has done so is a different question, and the UK, it is common ground, did not do so by extending the Patents Act 1977 to the purposes in the second part of Article 56(1)(a), i.e. production of energy from the water, current and winds (it did assert the rights under Article 56 for the production of wind energy for different purposes under the Energy Act 2004).

147.

Finally, Article 85 says as follows:

Article 85

Tunnelling

This Part does not prejudice the right of the coastal State to exploit the subsoil by means of tunnelling, irrespective of the depth of water above the subsoil.

Analysis

148.

As I have said above, this boils down to a short question of whether installation of the towers for the Haliade-X turbines constitutes “activities connected with the exploration of, or the exploitation of the natural resources of, the shore or bed of waters to which this section applies or the subsoil beneath it” within the meaning of the PA98 given the context I have just now described.

149.

Siemens says its argument in favour is fortified by, in particular, Article 80’s clarification that the provisions apply to structures and installation, and by the reference in Article 85 to tunnelling. Neither of these materially helps it, in my view. They are there to ensure that things ancillary to exploiting the resources in question and the subsoil are covered. If they were not, the argument might be made that exploiting the resources of the seabed is within the state’s rights but erecting structures is not, even if the latter were necessary to the former.

150.

So these clarifications do not change the basic purpose or activities required and do not, in my view, require anything other than their ordinary meaning to be given to the words of s. 11(2)(a) PA98.

151.

In my view, GE’s acts at Dogger Bank would not be “exploitation” of the kind required (“exploration” is not relied on and would obviously be a much weaker argument). No “natural resources” as referred to are exploited. What is taken advantage of is the mere existence of the sea bed and subsoil beneath it. It does not make sense to say that the resource of some rock is just that it is rock.

152.

One can of course understand that taking living or non-living resources like hydrocarbons or seaweed and doing something with them is “exploitation”, but drilling out some rock and doing nothing with it is not, in my view.

Kit of parts

153.

Siemens argued that having all the parts of the Haliade-X at the dock in the UK, but not assembled, infringed claim 1 of the Patent by virtue of being a “kit of parts”.

154.

This point is not necessary to the outcome of this action for multiple reasons, namely that the Patent is invalid, that Siemens has failed on the “of the rotor hub” point, and that Siemens could prevail on claim 9 had the only obstacle in its way been that the Haliade-X is not fully assembled in the UK.

155.

That being so, I decline to decide the kit of parts point. It is basically a matter of law, but one of some complexity where it would be necessary to decide whether to follow the same course as two decisions of the Courts of the Netherlands and of Germany, or to prefer the analysis of Arnold J (as he then was) in Virgin v. Delta [2010] EWHC 3094 (Pat).

156.

Arnold J’s decision was that it was arguable that dealing in a complete kit of parts can be an infringement in some circumstances, but that on the facts before him there was only an incomplete kit. The Court of Appeal reversed his decision in the result, not on the basis of the legal analysis but because on the facts it held that the claim scope was different from that which he had identified (the seating system claimed having to be suitable for a plane, not actually on a plane). That meant that his finding that infringement by a complete kit of parts was arguable concluded the matter, because it was a summary judgment application and not the trial.

157.

Although, as I have said, Arnold J was only considering whether a complete kit of parts was arguably an infringement, his own analysis was that it probably ought not to be, because such an approach was unnecessary and inconsistent with the territorial nature of patents, in particular the fact that for “means essential” infringement there is a double territoriality requirement (see [131] and [133]). What led him to conclude that the point was arguable were decisions of the foreign courts.

158.

This is a subtle and difficult point. It might be tempting to follow the Courts of other major EPC/CPC jurisdictions (as Jacob LJ suggested could be the right course in another context concerning infringement under CPC-originating provisions in Grimme Landmaschinenfabrik v Scott [2010] EWCA Civ 1110), but I am not sufficiently confident that I have the full picture. In particular, and intending no disrespect to the judges in the relevant decisions or to the researches of Counsel in this case, I think Arnold J’s points about territoriality are important and would need more research. For example, there have been, as I understand it, divided opinions in German law in the context of second medical use patents about whether there can be infringement in Germany if the invention is not put into effect there (because the drug is packaged for the second use, but then exported).

159.

So this point would be better determined in a case where it is decisive and all the relevant legal materials are to hand.

Conclusion on the infringement questions

160.

My conclusions are:

i)

Siemens succeeds on the “integral” point on claims 1 and 9.

ii)

GE succeeds on the “of the rotor hub” point and its analogue in claim 9.

iii)

Because of point ii) the Haliade-X (even when fully assembled) and its rotor hub are therefore not within the scope of the claims and there can be no infringement.

iv)

GE also succeeds on the Dogger Bank territoriality point.

v)

I decline to decide the kit of parts point.

The anticipation case over Loeschner is based on a squeeze. GE’s main case is that the Haliade-X does not infringe; in that case it accepts that there is no anticipation. But it says that if the Haliade-X does infringe, then there is anticipation because:

Pozzoli 4, assessment

204.

In assessing Stander, I will bear in mind the characteristics of the notional skilled person. In particular, they are diligent and careful, but not inventive. Their approach to Stander would be systematic and they would read the whole article.

205.

They would be open to the idea of looking at the references, but only if it were obvious to do that. An issue which starkly divided the parties before me was whether the skilled person would read reference 6, Klinger. There is a danger of answering that question with an undue and artificial focus brought about by Figure 3 being at the heart of the current dispute; the skilled person would not know that. Also, although the skilled person is diligent and careful, it is not legitimate to argue that they would by definition look at every reference. Apart from being unrealistic, such an approach might enable combining references in a way which was not in fact obvious and which would introduce hindsight.

206.

Consistently with my findings on CGK, the skilled person would know that configurations A1, A2 and B2 were in wide use. The skilled person would recognise them and be interested in them and what Stander had to say about them. But it would be a non sequitur to say that the skilled person would therefore not be interested in the other configurations. He or she would analyse them in a careful way, but without invention. Siemens’ argument that the skilled person would confine themselves to the CGK configurations has something of the Brugger fallacy about it. It is rather a concealed argument that progressing those configurations was the most obvious thing to do, so nothing else would be obvious.

The skilled person’s analysis would include thinking about whether Stander was teaching that the other configurations were in fact real designs, or just concepts. The skilled person would look differently at a pure paper proposal as opposed to a real design. In the former case, the skilled person would be more cautious but I do not believe they would reject it out of hand. They would be very interested in the configurations shown in the small drawings in either case.

214.

The Vensys 70 design is shown on page 12. In my view the skilled person would find it easy to see that this is configuration B4. The skilled person would see that it is called Vensys 70 while the right hand column in Table I of Stander says that B4 is from Gensys. I find that the skilled person would think this (i.e. the entry in Table I) was a slip. They would not know exactly how the slip had happened. They would see that the design on the upper left of page 17 was attributed to Genesys and that it was configuration B1. It is quite possible, though not necessary to my analysis, that they would think the names Vensys and Gensys were accidentally swapped around in Table I. They would also see configuration A2 attributed to “Vensys/Goldwind/Siemens” at the lower left on the same page (whereas Table I names only Goldwind and Siemens).

215.

I find that the skilled person would conclude that the Vensys 70 on page 12 correlated to the Vensys design in entry number 2 in the table on page 6 (for which one option is a rotor diameter of 70). That would support the conclusion, which they would anyway have regarded as likely from Stander itself, that configuration B4 was in use at a modest, possibly prototype level. Allowing for the configuration being misnamed in Table I, this would also fit with their CGK about Vensys as a company. They would appreciate that Vensys’ market penetration was very modest compared with Enercon and Goldwind. This would of course be a relevant consideration for them, but it would not dent their appreciation that configuration B4 was a real design and not a paper proposal.

216.

Counsel for Siemens argued that since the peer reviewers of Stander did not cross-check Table I against Klinger, nor would the skilled person. I do not think this is logical and although I think it is beside the point, I reject it. One cannot know if the reviewers looked at Klinger. They ought to have done, but then they ought not to have allowed the errors in Table I through in any event. All one can say is that for some reason they made a number of careless errors in matters of detail and their review did not match the unimaginative but very careful approach of the notional skilled person.

217.

I return to consider what the skilled person would make of the configurations in Stander. Prof Slocum’s evidence was, as I have said, that Table I was poorly put together and had errors but that the skilled person would nonetheless analyse the configurations in Figure 3 (I have also recorded that he accepted that he had himself misunderstood some of the references in the right hand column in Table I, for example in relation to MTorres). I accept the overall thrust of his evidence on this. The skilled person’s reaction would be that Table I was, surprisingly for an academic article, very sloppily put together. That is not the same as technical information being wrong. They would still think that Figure 3 was useful and interesting and likely to present sound ideas, but ones that would need analysis.

218.

Prof Slocum explained what the skilled person would make of each of the configurations in a section of his second report which I found to be careful and systematic but not to involve any invention.

219.

First, he said that the skilled person would think about the single bearing arrangements but would recognise that they would have limitations for bigger turbines. He acknowledged that the single bearing configurations included very well known ones. Siemens said it was illogical to suggest that the CGK configurations would not be of interest, but that is not a fair representation of what Prof Slocum was saying. He was saying they were of less interest for bigger turbines. That is sensible and supported by Stander itself, as I have mentioned above.

220.

Second, he said that the triple bearing arrangements, and some of the double bearing arrangements were too “academic”. He had later to correct himself in relation to e.g. MTorres when he found that it was a company actually working in the field, but that is not important in my view. In any event he went through configurations B6-B8 and C1-C2 to give specific reasons why they were unattractive. He also gave reasons for disliking configuration B5, which has the further blemish that it is not in Table I but is in Figure 3.

221.

As to the rest of the configurations, he did a similar analysis and said that B1 would be of interest for taking forward, that B2 and B3 would not be (in his third report he very fairly changed his mind on B2 having considered Dr Santos’ evidence, but this does not affect my thinking), and that B4 would.

222.

It is worth setting out what he wrote about B4:

223.

At this stage he was proceeding on the basis of Gensys’ becoming Vensys as the reason for the names given in Table I, and that the skilled person would know as much (and he said so in footnote 32, which I have not reproduced above). I have reached a different conclusion about the skilled person knowing that specifically and my view is only that the skilled person would have concluded that configuration B4 was a real design as proposed to a mere paper proposal. However, this does not materially undermine his evidence.

224.

Prof Slocum’s analysis of the configurations from Figure 3 is approximately at the level of the teeter-totter, although it is qualitative and not quantitative.

225.

Prof Slocum said that the skilled person would do the quantitative teeter-totter analysis of the Annex of his first report “to position and size the bearings” of configurations B1 and B4.

226.

In relation to the specific details of this analysis, Siemens made a number of points. Three main ones were that:

i)

Design B4 might not in fact intend the upwind bearing to be inside the hub, because all that Table I said was that it was upwind of the stator. I reject this; the drawings in Figure 3 are quite careful and where it was intended to show bearings at the edge of the hub, that was done. The member going into the hub to mount the bearing inside it (what is called the annular member in the Patent) is clearly deliberate. If the skilled person did look at Klinger, this is even clearer.

ii)

Prof Slocum did not in fact do the teeter-totter analysis for Stander configuration B4 but only for Hua, and he referenced the latter when defending his evidence on Stander in cross-examination. Siemens argued that as a result he was unable to show that following up configuration B4 would result in a bearing inside the hub. I reject this, too. The point on Hua and Stander was very similar and was simply: would numerical analysis show that moving the bearing nearer the centre of mass would help? The example analysis with Hua showed that it would (which is hardly surprising since it is simple mechanics, and true, being the basis of the Patent). It was perfectly plausible for Prof Slocum to say that he could read across from Hua to Stander in this way.

iii)

Prof Slocum had muddled up the centre of stiffness and the centre of mass. I do not accept this. He refers to them in footnote 31 of his second report and his analysis of configuration B4 seemed consistent with what he said there. He certainly did not accept any such criticism.

227.

Dr Santos’ evidence was chiefly to the following effect (I say nothing about the errors in Table I at this stage since Prof Slocum accepted their existence and I have already covered it above, but I do note that Dr Santos did not in his written evidence say that the errors were such that the skilled person would discard Stander):

i)

The skilled person would not give Stander much weight because its authors were not well known. I agree this is a factor but only a minor one. The skilled person would read the document with interest (as the law requires) and primarily assess it on its technical merit.

ii)

Stander has a leaning towards single bearing configurations. I do not think that is really complete. It recognises that they are conventional but says they could have problems of scalability in moving to bigger turbines.

iii)

The skilled person would only be interested in progressing the CGK configurations A1, A2, and B2. I did not find that convincing for reasons given above.

iv)

That the skilled person would not analyse all the configurations in Table 3 because it would be too much work. I accept that they would not analyse them all, and have already found that they would not be interested in the triple bearing arrangements. But I do not consider that the point has force in relation to configuration B4, again for reasons given above, and I do not accept that the level of analysis would be especially great, or any material deterrent.

v)

In relation to B4 specifically, that it might imperil the air gap. Prof Slocum recognised this as a factor in the evidence I have referred to above but said that it was a trade-off. I agree that the skilled person would think it was something to watch out for and is one reason for doing a more quantitative analysis. On the other hand the skilled person would see there definitely was an advantage in B4 in terms of reducing the load on the bearing.

228.

Although I am sure he was sincere in giving it, I did not find Dr Santos’ evidence at all convincing. In particular, I thought his view that the skilled person would be blinkered and unwilling to consider anything new was unrealistic in the circumstances of this art, and generally.

229.

I therefore uphold the attack of obviousness over Stander on the basis that progressing configuration B4 was one of a relatively small number of obvious routes forward from it. It was not suggested that any dependent claim could help Siemens and, as I have said, although the use of a mechanical bearing and of a press fit for configuration B4 were not disclosed to the anticipation standard they were clearly very obvious options once the decision was taken to progress configuration B4.

INSUFFICIENCY

230.

GE argued that the Patent is insufficient because its claims cover (or, on its case, are limited to) turbines in which the annular member is truly integral with the outer ring of the bearing; and further, the claims cover very large turbines. GE says that that means that the claims cover things that are not enabled.

The allegations and the facts

231.

I have sketched out the nature of GE’s argument. There is rather more detail to it, as I will now explain:

i)

It is common ground that the claims of the Patent would cover the situation where the annular member is truly integral with the outer ring of the bearing. GE says that that is always necessary to fall within the claims while Siemens says it is an option.

ii)

The claims of the Patent do not require any particular size of turbine, but the specification stresses the particular usefulness of the invention for larger sizes. I have touched on the relevant passages when narrating the specification.

iii)

GE argued, supported by Prof Slocum, that it would not be possible to make a large rolling element bearing outer ring that was truly integral with the annular member.

iv)

The details of why that is so do not matter, but in very brief outline it has to do with accomplishing a very large casting and at the same time being able to achieve the very demanding material qualities and tolerances of the outer ring.

v)

The reason the details do not matter is that Dr Santos did not challenge Prof Slocum’s evidence about the practical difficulties. His response, and Siemens’ answer, was that the skilled person would not be worried about the difficulties because they would know they could use press fitting or the like. So they would not think they needed to make a very large, truly integral assembly.

vi)

GE does not challenge the factual proposition that a press-fit solution would work, but says that there is still insufficiency in law because the claim covers (or is limited to) the truly integral option.

vii)

The specification also discusses casting the annular member in one piece with the rotor hub itself. This, Prof Slocum said, would be even more difficult. Again, he was not challenged, but again Siemens’ answer was that a press fit solution would be used. So I did not think this changed the overall argument.

viii)

In closing submissions, GE argued that the attack applied both to rolling element bearings and to sliding/journal bearings. As I read the pleadings and evidence, GE’s case was not really about the latter. It does not matter to my analysis, though.

Legal standard

232.

GE argued that the decision of the Supreme Court in Regeneron v Kymab [2020] UKSC 27, particularly at [56], was applicable to this kind of attack. I was also referred to the discussion of that decision in the judgment of Birss LJ (sitting at first instance) in Illumina v Latvia MGI [2021] EWHC 57 (Pat), at [257]-[259] and [276]-[279].

Analysis

233.

Since the facts are not in dispute and since I find that the Patent is invalid over Stander for obviousness, I will be brief in my analysis.

234.

I agree with GE that although not expressly mentioned in the claim, size is a relevant range in the Regeneron sense. It is of the essence of the invention that moving the bearing inside the rotor hub allows bigger turbines to be made.

235.

But I do not accept that the bearing type and/or the requirement to be truly integral is a relevant range. It would be a matter of indifference to the skilled reader of the Patent, so far as achieving the benefits promised, that they needed to use a press fit for larger turbines (whether with rolling element bearings, or, so far as it mattered, journal bearings). Of course there would be technical work to do whatever they chose, but it would not have an impact on the benefit of the invention.

236.

So I think in the present case the allegation is close to the chocolate tea pot referred to by Birss LJ in Illumina and quite different from the size of insert considered in Regeneron. As Birss LJ said at (in particular) [278] and [279](iv) in Illumina, any descriptive language will cover a range of things and that in itself does not mean that it constitutes a relevant range in the Regeneron sense. Usually, descriptive or functional language will not cause a problem.

237.

I also found it helpful to this analysis to think more generally about what the skilled person’s reaction to the specification would be in the respects relevant to this argument. It would be “Yes, I see that putting the bearing inside the hub will help with bigger turbines. I know I won’t be able to make a monolithic annular member/bearing outer ring at that scale but I don’t care because I can just use press fitting”.

238.

Seen in this way, I think that the insufficiency allegation is not consistent with, and indeed damages, GE’s position on claim interpretation. It would not make sense for the skilled person to think that “integral” was limited to truly integral and excluded press-fitting when that was exactly what the skilled person would think was necessary to make the invention work at the large scale to which it was specifically directed.

239.

I therefore reject the insufficiency attack.

CONCLUSIONS

240.

My conclusions are:

i)

The Patent is invalid for obviousness over Stander.

ii)

The obviousness and anticipation attacks over Loeschner, the anticipation attack over Stander, and the insufficiency allegation all fail.

iii)

As to infringement:

a)

Neither the fully assembled Haliade-X nor its hub fall within the scope of claims 1 or 9 (respectively) of the Patent.

b)

There is therefore no infringement.

c)

Dogger Bank is not within the territorial ambit of the Patents Act 1977 so GE’s acts there would not be infringements anyway.

d)

But had I been wrong about the meaning of claim 9, it would have been infringed by GE’s acts in relation to the Haliade-X rotor hub in mainland UK, in particular at Able Seaton.

iv)

I do not need to decide “kit of parts” and decline to do so.

241.

I direct that time for seeking permission to appeal shall not run until after the hearing on the form of Order (or the making of such Order if it is agreed). I draw attention to paragraph 19.1 of the Patents Court Guide, which says that a hearing on the form of Order should take place within 28 days of hand down. In the present case, 28 days from hand down will be 28 December 2022 so the hearing should take place as early as possible in the new Term in January 2023.
